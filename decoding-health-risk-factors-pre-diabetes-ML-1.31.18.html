Motivation¶

According to the American Diabetes Association, the total cost of diabetes in 2012 was 245 billion. Direct medical costs were 176 billion, and productivity losses associated with the disease were estimated at 69 billion (DIABETES CARE, VOLUME 36, APRIL 2013). In a recent study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association, Diabetes was ranked the highest of all conditions in personal healthcare spending. The study estimated the analyzed growth rate of Diabetes spending at 6% over the years 1996 to 2013 (JAMA December 27, 2016 Volume 316, Number 24). The Center for Disease Control and Prevention state that Diabetes is the leading cause of kidney failure, nontramatic limb amputation, and new cases of blindness in US adults. It is also a major cause of heart disease and stoke and is the 7th leading cause of death in the US (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. National diabetes fact sheet: national estimates and general information on diabetes and prediabetes in the United States, 2011. Atlanta, GA: U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2011).

As these numbers show, Diabetes is a significant public health problem that requires immediate attention. While many medical studies exist, the rate of Diabetes continues to increase as does the cost of care.

Therfore, the questions raised in this post are: _What can data science do to increase our understanding of the risk factors associated with Diabetes? How can those insights be used to help Diabetesx patients and their care givers reduce these risks and increase their quality of life?

The Pima Indians of Arizona have been the subject of many medical studies over the years due to the high prevalence of diabetes and pre-diabetes. The most recent study is an ongoing clinical trial studying pre-diabetes sponsored by the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK) that began in June 2006.

An ongoing debate centers around the question of "nature vs. nuture" in determining the causes of the high prevalence of diabetes among the Pima Indians and if it can be prevented through behavioral changes (see links below for examples).

In this post, I analyze the Pima Indians Diabetes dataset from the UCI ML Repository to explore pre-diabetes risk factors using Machine Learning and Deep Learning pipelines for data preparation, feature extraction, model evaluation and algorithm tuning. Imputation of Missing Values*¶

Machine Learning algorithms can be very useful for predicting missing values in data. Given the potential importance of insulin levels and skin fold thickness as risk factors for Pre Diabetes, I will attempt to create regression models to predict the missing values of skin fold thickness and insulin levels using the other attributes in the dataset using ensemble methods. Given the low percentage of missing in plasma glucose, diastolic blood pressure, and BMI I will use a univariate strategy for imputation such as imputing the mean, median, or mode of the attribute.

Skin Fold Thickness and Insulin Levels Imputation Models*¶

To maximize information regarding the joint distribution of Pre Diabetes risk factors, all cases with missing values are removed from the training and validation samples. The underlying assumption for removing cases with missing values is that these measurements are Missing At Random, i.e., the distributions in the new sample will be similar to the original sample. This will be checked.

For this analysis, the focus is the two attributes that have the greatest number of missing values: tricep skin fold thickness and 2 hour serum insulin level. First we model skin fold thickness and then model insulin levels. The goal is to find the best model among an ensemble of models for imputing missing values in each attribute.

*For more information on setting up ensembles in Python see Jason Brownlee. Ensemble Machine Learning Algorithms in Python with scikit-learn. Machine Learning Mastery. June 3, 2016

Partial Dependency Plots for Identifying Pre Diabetes Risk Factors*¶

To examine the relative importance of different risk factors in Pre Diabetes I will use an additive Gradient Boosting Machine (GBM). Gradient Boosting Machines and its ensemble form eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) are one of the most power learning ideas in machine learning.* The learning concept behind boosting and additive trees is to combine a set of weaker models into a committee of experts. GBM is a sequence of simple models that are weighted and summed together to form the committee of experts for prediction. Each model in the GBM sequence is tasked with predicting the error of the model that preceeds it.

To understand the influence of each attribute in a GBM requires graphing the predicted values with each attribute individually while averaging out the effects of the other attributes. This graph is called a partial dependence plot.

*For a full explanation of interpretation of Partial Dependence Plots in Boosting and Additive Trees see Hastie T, Tibshirani R, Friedman J. Elements of Statistical Learning: Data Mining, Inference, and Prediction. 2nd edition. New York (NY): Springer-Verlag; 2009. Chapter 10.13.2, Partial Dependence Plots; p. 369-384.

Discussion¶

The partial dependence plots shows a threshold effect between insulin levels and the pedigree measure. There appears to be a subpopulation of Pima Indian women with a pedigree score higher than .6 with elevated insulin levels who are at greater risk of developing Pre Diabetes than other women in a similar cohort. Further investigation is needed to confirm this finding but if the finding holds it points to an important public health insight: women with a family history of diabetes should have their insulin levels monitored periodically as an early warning of the risk of developing Pre Diabetes.
